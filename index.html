Proposition: Black and Hispanic civilians are disproportionately targeted by police force complaints compared to White civilians.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Using Absolute Counts Instead of Rates: Focused on total complaints rather than normalizing by population size to emphasize differences by race. Score: -1.5 (Deceptive) — Disparity without proper context.
  • Contrasting & Intentional Coloring: The tallest bar, which is used to represent the "bad" group, is colored red, and the "good" group is colored green. Score: -1.0 (Mildly Deceptive) — Makes the graph emotionally charged.
  • Highlighting Annotation: Draws attention to the large disparity in the data. Score: +0.5 (Earnest) — Factual, but tries to paint a narrative.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Normalized Metric (Complaints per Substantiation): Focused on efficiency of accountability rather than raw counts, which shifts viewer perception toward uniformity. Score: +1 (Earnest) Promotes fair comparison but still selectively reframes the issue.
  • Calm Color Palette: Chose neutral blue tones for neutrality in an atempt to decrease emotional appeal from the data. Score: +1.5 (Earnest) Encourages rational interpretation without emotional bias.
  • Reference Line at National Average: Added dashed red line to emphasize that all groups perform better than national norms. Score: +0.5 (Slightly Persuasive) Contextualizes data positively but downplays intergroup differences.

Final Reflection

Creating these two visualizations showed us how much presentation affects interpretation, even when the data is the same. For the first chart, which supports the idea that there are racial disparities in NYPD complaints, we focused on showing total complaint counts. Using strong red and green colors made the differences feel more dramatic and emotional, which helped make the point but also left out population context. It was interesting to see how easy it is to make a visualization persuasive by just changing a few design choices. The second chart took the opposite stance by reframing the data as complaints per substantiated case. We used calm blue colors, a balanced layout, and a reference line to make everything feel more neutral and consistent. It looked fairer, but we realized that it still guides how people read the data by focusing on a different metric. What felt “objective” at first was really just another way of shaping the story. Overall, this project made us think differently about what ethical visualization means. It is not just about being accurate with the data but also being honest about how design choices influence perception. We don’t think there is a strict line between persuasive and misleading visuals, but the intent matters. If the goal is to clarify and inform, persuasion can be ethical. When the goal is to hide or distort, that is when it crosses into deception.
